Morning-after pill without mandatory consultation: what changes

Di Valerie Linc · Published on 30 April 2026

The Swiss National Council approves the sale of the morning-after pill without mandatory consultation. Here's what changes for women in Ticino and Switzerland.

Contesto

In brief - Swiss National Council approves over-the-counter sale of morning-after pill - 113 votes in favor, 73 against - Motion by Corina Gredig (LDP) ## Key facts - What: Over-the-counter sale of morning-after pill - When: April 30, 2026 - Where: Switzerland - Who: Swiss National Council - Vote: 113 in favor, 73 against The Swiss National Council has sparked significant debate with the approval of the sale of the morning-after pill without mandatory specialist consultation. The motion, presented by Corina Gredig of the Liberal Green Party (LDP), was approved on Wednesday with 113 votes in favor, 73 against, and 2 abstentions. The proposal aims to reclassify emergency contraceptives into a lower dispensing category, making them more accessible. Gredig criticized the mandatory consultation, calling it a 'deterrent effect' that often leads to feelings of shame. 'Every adult and responsible woman should be able to decide freely and without unnecessary barriers whether to take this pill,' she argued. The proposal also includes the possibility of selling the morning-after pill in drugstores, given the perception of a low risk level. ### Implications for Ticino The canton of Ticino, with its proximity to Italy and the constant flow of cross-border workers, could see a significant impact from this decision. Women working and residing in Ticino could benefit from easier access to emergency contraceptives, reducing waiting times and bureaucratic barriers. ### Comparison with other European countries Compared to other European countries, access to the morning-after pill in Switzerland is more regulated. Analysis of Practical Implications The approval of Gredig's motion could have a significant impact on women seeking emergency contraception in Switzerland, particularly in the Canton of Ticino. Simplifying the access process could reduce waiting times and bureaucratic barriers, making it easier for women to obtain the medication in emergency situations. ### Comparison with the Current Situation Currently, women in Switzerland must undergo a mandatory counseling interview before obtaining the morning-after pill. This process can be lengthy and, according to Gredig, can have a deterrent effect. The proposed over-the-counter sale could eliminate this barrier, making the medication more accessible. ### Possible Scenarios If the motion is approved by the Council of States, women could purchase the morning-after pill without undergoing a counseling interview. This could lead to an increase in the use of the medication, but also to a potential increase in side effects if adequate counseling is not provided. ### Impact on the Healthcare System Health Minister Elisabeth Baume-Schneider has expressed concerns about patient safety. Over-the-counter sale of the morning-after pill could increase the burden on the healthcare system, as women may need medical assistance to manage potential side effects or interactions with other medications. ### Economic Considerations Selling the morning-after pill in drugstores could reduce costs for pharmacies, which currently must dedicate time and resources to counseling interviews.
