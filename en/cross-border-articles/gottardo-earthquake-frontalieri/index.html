Researchers trigger microseisms in the Gotthard massif. What we know and the implications for cross-border workers in Ticino.

Contesto

In brief - Researchers trigger controlled earthquake in the Gotthard - Microseisms recorded in the BedrettoLab - Unique data for earthquake physics ## Key facts - What: Controlled earthquake in the Gotthard massif - When: April 29, 2026 - Where: BedrettoLab, Ticino - Who: RWTH Aachen University, ETH Zurich, INGV - Amount: Microseisms not perceptible on the surface For the first time, researchers have triggered a controlled earthquake in the Gotthard massif, Ticino. The experiment, conducted by ETH Zurich, RWTH Aachen University, and the Italian National Institute of Geophysics and Volcanology (INGV), injected high-pressure water into a fault zone near the BedrettoLab. The goal was to alter the stress between the rocks and trigger microseisms. These tiny earthquakes were not perceptible on the surface but were recorded by hundreds of hypersensitive sensors placed near the fault. According to RWTH Aachen University, the devices are so sensitive that they recorded the earthquake that occurred on April 20 in Japan directly in the BedrettoLab. Thanks to this experiment, researchers have been able to measure an earthquake at its origin, obtaining unique data on earthquake physics. The project, named 'FEAR', should allow for improved long-term earthquake predictability. ### Implications for cross-border workers Cross-border workers living and working in Ticino may wonder about the implications of this experiment. Although the triggered microseisms are not perceptible on the surface, the research could lead to a better understanding of earthquakes and, potentially, greater seismic safety in the region. This could influence regulations and safety measures for construction and infrastructure, improving the quality of life and safety of residents and cross-border workers. ###...
