Bonaparte gull spotted in Switzerland: first time in Ticino (cross-border guide)

Di Valerie Linc · Published on 4 May 2026

A Bonaparte gull, a rare American specimen, has been spotted for the first time in Switzerland, causing a stir among enthusiasts.

Contesto

In brief - Bonaparte's gull spotted in Switzerland for the first time - Dozens of enthusiasts gathered to admire it - Future of the specimen uncertain ## Key facts - What: Sighting of a Bonaparte's gull - When: Friday, May 2, 2026 - Where: Municipality of Préverenges, Vaud - Who: Ornithological Circle of Lausanne (COL) - Amount: Not specified The Vaud municipality of Préverenges experienced a historic moment from an ornithological perspective over the weekend: a Bonaparte's gull, the American cousin of the common gull, was observed for the first time in Switzerland. Dozens of enthusiasts gathered to admire it. The bird was discovered on Friday evening, as announced by the Ornithological Circle of Lausanne (COL) in a statement today. The news spread on Saturday, causing a stir, but the rare specimen disappeared again on Sunday, only to reappear this morning. The young gull likely ended up in Europe due to the weather. 'American migrants are currently in full migration phase and are returning from south to north. If caught in strong winds, they can end up on the other side of the Ocean, on the wrong continent,' explained Terry Guillaume of the COL to Keystone-ATS. While it is relatively frequent to spot American species in Switzerland, this is the first time for a Bonaparte's gull. The future of the specimen remains uncertain: either it will settle in and stay in place, where common gulls also nest, or it will continue its migration north. However, it might also realize the mistake and attempt the Atlantic crossing, said Terry Guillaume. The Bonaparte's gull nests in Canada and Alaska and spends the winter in the southern United States or Mexico. It resembles the gulls of our latitudes, but among other things, it is smaller. ### Implications for ornithology enthusiasts...
