In brief - Fire-resistant panels made with watermelon seeds - Discovery by the Polytechnic of Zurich - Recyclable and self-extinguishing material ## Key facts - What: Fire-resistant panels with watermelon seeds - When: May 2, 2026 - Where: Federal Polytechnic of Zurich - Who: Ronny Kürsteiner, PhD student at the Polytechnic of Zurich - Amount: Not yet specified The tragedy of Crans-Montana has brought attention back to a crucial issue: the choice of construction materials that can make a difference in fire protection. Now, a promising proposal comes from the Federal Polytechnic of Zurich: fire-resistant panels made with watermelon seed powder. Mixed with sawdust, urea, struvite, and water, the powder of watermelon seeds can become the base of a material that takes a long time to catch fire. Not only that: according to the researchers, the composite can even extinguish itself. ### How does it work? Ronny Kürsteiner, a PhD student at the Polytechnic of Zurich, explains the mechanism. The behavior of the material, he reports, depends partly on the struvite: when heated, it decomposes and releases gas. This process has a double effect: on the one hand, it removes heat because decomposition itself requires it; on the other hand, it helps to remove oxygen from the surface. In this way, explains Kürsteiner, 'a protective layer of non-flammable gas is formed.' The discovery was born from the work of researchers at the Polytechnic of Zurich in collaboration with the Swiss Federal Laboratories for Materials Science and Technology (EMPA). The role of watermelon seeds is linked to their enzyme, which allows the crystallization of struvite together with the sawdust.
