Frequently Asked Questions
What is the penalty for attempted aggravated theft in Switzerland?
Swiss legislation provides for penalties of up to 3 years in prison for attempted aggravated theft, plus fines of up to 10,000 francs.
What is the average value of items recovered in cases of cross-border aggravated theft in Switzerland-Italy?
According to the available data, the average value of objects recovered in such cases is around 1,500 Swiss francs, as evidenced in the Sargans episode.
What happens if a foreign minor is apprehended for cross-border theft between Switzerland and Italy?
In Switzerland, foreign minors involved in crimes such as theft are subject to protection measures in accordance with the Youth Protection Act. They can be entrusted to social services or specialized facilities, with prison sentences of up to 6 months and fines of up to 10,000 francs, but the main objective remains education and reintegration. Italy, on the other hand, applies harsher penalties, with prison sentences between 2 and 6 years.
How are seized items handled in cases of cross-border theft between Switzerland and Italy?
Seized items, such as burglary tools or stolen goods, are typically retained as evidence in court proceedings. If left unclaimed, they can be destroyed, auctioned or donated to charity. In cross-border cases, cooperation between Swiss and Italian authorities also makes it possible to trace the origin of the stolen goods.
What are the repatriation procedures for foreign minors involved in crimes in Switzerland?
Foreign minors apprehended in Switzerland for crimes, such as theft, can only be repatriated after the family and social situation in the country of origin has been ascertained. The authorities work with social services and NGOs to ensure safe reintegration. Return is subject to an assessment of the best interests of the child.
