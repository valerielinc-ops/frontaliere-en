Short work week in Switzerland: fewer hours, more productivity

Di Valerie Linc · Published on 26 April 2026

A Swiss experiment shows that working fewer hours for the same salary reduces absences and increases employee motivation

Contesto

TL;DR - Swiss companies test 4-day workweek with same salary - Employees report improved health and productivity - Cross-border workers may benefit from extra free time ## Key facts - Cosa: Test della settimana lavorativa di 4 giorni - Quando: Sperimentazione durata 6 mesi, iniziata prima di aprile - Dove: Romandia e Zurigo - Chi: Azienda di servizi amministrativi, NGO, piccola impresa e banca - Importo: Riduzione da 42 a 34 ore settimanali - Scadenza: Collaborazione con Università Berna per studio scientifico - Impatto: Aumento motivazione e ottimizzazione processi ## Experiment in Switzerland An increasing number of Swiss companies are experimenting with a four-day workweek while maintaining the same salary. This approach, aimed at countering the steady rise in sick leave over the past decade, has been tested by an administrative services company operating in Romandy and Zurich. For six months, around forty employees trialed the shorter week, with positive results for both health and productivity. ### How the Experiment Works Each worker had the freedom to choose how to reorganize their time. For example, one employee opted for an extra day off each week while keeping the same salary. 'It's a life-changer,' they enthuse, explaining that this flexibility allows them to 'not waste Saturday' on household chores. 'By moving them to Friday, you have the whole weekend free: it's really cool!' ## Positive Balance Although the staff returned to a 42-hour week at the beginning of April, the experiment's balance is extremely positive. 'We had excellent feedback on the balance between personal and professional quality of life,' states a collaborator. Dettagli operativi

Implications for cross-border workers The introduction of the four-day workweek in Switzerland could have significant implications for cross-border workers in Ticino. With the reduction in working hours, employees may have more time for personal and family activities, improving their quality of life. This could make the Swiss job market even more attractive to Italian workers. ### Comparisons with the previous situation Before the introduction of the short week, employees worked 42 hours a week. With the new model, many have reduced their hours to 34, maintaining the same salary. This has allowed for better time management and increased efficiency at work. Cross-border workers could benefit from this flexibility, having more time for household chores and family. ## Practical scenarios Imagine a cross-border worker employed by one of the companies that have adopted the short week. This worker could have an extra day off each week, allowing them to spend more time with family in Italy or dedicate time to other personal activities. The reduction in working hours could also lead to a decrease in stress and an improvement in mental health, with long-term benefits for productivity and job satisfaction. ### Procedure for companies Companies interested in experimenting with the short week must collaborate with institutions such as the Bern University of Applied Sciences to scientifically analyze the effects of this reduction in working hours. It is important to verify if the company is participating in similar experiments or is willing to consider this option. Additionally, workers can consult the Bern University of Applied Sciences to obtain information on ongoing studies and the benefits of the four-day workweek. ### Concrete steps 1.
