Domestic violence: Zurich model proves convincing

Di Valerie Linc · Published on 27 April 2026

The pilot project for real-time electronic monitoring to combat domestic violence has yielded positive results. The goal is to extend it nationwide by 2027.

Contesto

In brief - Pilot project Zurich: real-time electronic monitoring - Low costs: around 60,000 francs - Goal: national extension by 2027 ## Key facts - What: Pilot project of real-time electronic monitoring to combat domestic violence - When: Project launched on April 30, 2021 - Where: Canton of Zurich - Who: Intercantonal association led by Vassilis Venizelos - Amount: Total cost of around 60,000 francs Vassilis Venizelos, president of the intercantonal association, presented to RSI the results of the pilot project of real-time electronic monitoring to combat domestic violence. The project, carried out in the Canton of Zurich, demonstrated the effectiveness of active and dynamic monitoring at low costs. The total expenditure was around 60,000 francs, also thanks to the use of a private monitoring center. The project highlighted the importance of developing such measures on a national or, at least, intercantonal scale, as domestic violence does not recognize borders. A potential perpetrator can move from one canton to another, and for this reason, coordinated tools and common procedures are needed. In Zurich, about ten cases were monitored. It is difficult to determine how many episodes were actually prevented, but the project allowed to concretely evaluate the police's response capacity. A decisive element was the definition of the prohibition perimeters: in the first phase, a radius of one kilometer, the so-called Zurich model, was tested. Practical Analysis: Implications for Cross-Border Workers The pilot project for real-time electronic monitoring to combat domestic violence has significant implications for cross-border workers living and working in Ticino. Active and dynamic surveillance, with contained costs, represents a step forward in the fight against domestic violence, a phenomenon that knows no borders. Cross-border workers can benefit from greater security and protection, thanks to the collaboration between the Cantons and the use of coordinated tools and common procedures. The goal of creating a single national surveillance center by 2027 represents a further step towards greater consistency in the treatment of domestic violence cases. This means that victims of domestic violence, regardless of the Canton in which they are located, will be able to benefit from more effective and coordinated protection. Specific training for police officers on the technical aspects of the device, operational procedures, and information management is another key element to ensure the effectiveness of the system. ### Comparison with the Previous Situation Before the introduction of the pilot project for real-time electronic monitoring, the fight against domestic violence in Switzerland was characterized by a fragmentation of procedures and protective measures. Each Canton had its own regulations and methods of intervention, which made it difficult to guarantee uniform and coordinated protection for victims of domestic violence.
