Another Heavy Vote: Yes to a Transit Tax for Drivers Passing Through Switzerland Without Stopping (cross-border guide)

The National Council approves a motion that introduces a transit fee for those crossing Switzerland without stopping, aiming to ease congestion on main routes like the Gotthard.

At a time when cross-border traffic in Ticino continues to grow, Switzerland is preparing for a real revolution in motorway mobility. Recently, the National Council approved, with an overwhelming vote of 173 in favor and only 13 against, a motion presented by Councillor Marco Chiesa. This measure involves the introduction of a transit tax targeting drivers who cross the Confederation without stopping, meaning without significant pauses. The primary goal is to decongest key arteries such as the Gotthard and the Brogeda and Gaggiolo passes, which are often congested during peak periods like holidays and summer months. The proposal relies on an automatic detection system using cameras already installed at border crossings, such as Basel and Chiasso, which record license plates upon entry and exit. Dettagli operativi

The idea of taxing rapid transit stems from the desire to modulate traffic flow and reduce long queues at border crossings, especially during peak periods. The proposed tariffs are variable and based on several parameters, such as traffic congestion levels, time of crossing, and the day of the week. For example, during holiday weekends like Easter or Pentecost, costs could increase, encouraging drivers to plan their trips more carefully. The system draws inspiration from models already used by other countries, such as Austria with its license plate detection systems and Italy’s Pedemontana, which apply transit fees for passing without stopping. The monitoring technology, already installed at border points like Gaggiolo and Brogeda, detects license plates at entry and exit, calculating the transit time. If this duration falls below a set threshold, the fee is applied. Exemptions will be granted to Swiss residents and tourists staying in the country’s accommodations, who are not required to pay. The legislation also envisions incentives for those who plan their trips during less congested times. The tariff, still under development, could range between 5 and 15 Swiss francs, depending on traffic conditions and the period. Management of this tax will be entrusted to both canton and federal authorities, with continuous controls and real-time updates. Scheduled to come into force in 2026, the regulation fits into broader policies of sustainable mobility and cross-border traffic flow management, aiming to reduce pollution and improve quality of life in border areas. The main challenge lies in balancing traffic fluidity with the mobility needs of workers and tourists, without excessively penalizing daily travel. Frequently Asked Questions
When will the new transit tax come into effect for motorists who cross Switzerland without stopping?
The new transit tax is expected to take effect in 2026.
How will the exemptions for Swiss residents and tourists staying in accommodation facilities be managed?
The exemptions will be managed through an automatic detection system that checks the license plates and verifies the stay at Swiss accommodation facilities. Swiss residents and tourists with registration at such facilities will be automatically excluded from paying the transit fee.
How much will the new transit tax in Switzerland cost for those who pass without stopping?
The rate of the new transit tax in Switzerland is not yet defined in detail, but estimates indicate a possible variation between 5 and 15 Swiss francs. The exact amount will depend on factors such as the level of traffic congestion, the time of passage and the day of the week, with potentially higher fares during peak periods.
What are the detection systems used for the transit tax Switzerland?
Switzerland will use an automatic detection system using cameras already present at border crossings, such as in Basel and Chiasso. These cameras will record the license plates of incoming and outgoing vehicles, calculating the travel time to identify rapid transits without significant stops.
How can I avoid the new transit tax Switzerland?
To avoid the transit tax, drivers should plan their trips by avoiding rush hours and busy periods, such as holidays and weekends. Alternatively, you can consider staying in Switzerland for a significant period, thus becoming exempt from paying the rapid transit fee.
