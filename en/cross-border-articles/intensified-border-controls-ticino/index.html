Ticino Border: 'Radar' Checks for Cross-Border Workers (cross-border guide)

Di Valerie Linc · Published on 2 March 2026

A week of intensified checks at Ticino's border crossings, from Brogeda to Gaggiolo, aimed at tackling undeclared work and tax evasion. Here are the practical implications for cross-border commuters.

The Ticino border has become a hotspot for intensified inspections. As highlighted in Il Giornale del Ticino's headline, 'And where there are borders... a week of relentless “radar” checks', the Canton of Ticino has seen a surge in controls at its main border points, from Brogeda to Stabio-Gaggiolo, including Ponte Tresa and Chiasso. This crackdown, which has marked the past week, is not an isolated event but part of a broader Swiss strategy to combat issues such as undeclared work, tax evasion, and cross-border crime. Patrols from the Cantonal Police and Border Guards, often joined by specialists from the Federal Office for Customs and Border Security (FOCBS) and, in some cases, SUVA officials for social insurance checks, have been stationed at crossing points with a frequency and thoroughness above the usual. Dettagli operativi

The stepped-up checks at the Ticino border raise important practical and legal issues for cross-border workers. The inspections go far beyond simple identity verification. Officers are authorized to check the validity of the G Permit, essential for those working in Switzerland while residing in Italy, and to verify that the declared job matches the actual work performed—a crucial step to prevent undeclared or irregular employment. Regarding vehicles, checks focus on proper registration, valid third-party liability insurance (RC auto) for Switzerland, and compliance with Swiss road regulations. An Italian-plated vehicle driven by a cross-border worker must, in principle, be registered in the country of residence, but Swiss authorities may investigate improper or prolonged use in Switzerland, which could constitute a tax or customs violation. It’s worth remembering that, for consumer goods, there are strict duty-free import limits: for example, fuel (up to 25 liters in the tank plus 10 liters in a can per vehicle), as well as food, alcohol, and tobacco—any excess must be declared and taxed. Failure to respect these limits or omitting declarations can result in hefty fines proportional to the undeclared goods' value, and in serious cases, criminal proceedings for smuggling. The Department of Finance and Economy (DFE) of Canton Ticino, in cooperation with the Federal Customs Administration, constantly monitors these flows to ensure tax fairness. The new cross-border tax agreement, effective from 2024 for new hires and from 2026 for 'old' cross-border workers, introduces further complexities, with Switzerland retaining a larger share of taxes and requiring workers to declare income in Italy as well. Punti chiave

Faced with this intensification of border checks, cross-border workers should take a proactive approach to avoid unpleasant setbacks. The first—and most obvious—recommendation is crucial: always carry all required documents, making sure they are up to date. This includes your passport or ID card, a current G Permit, driver’s license, and vehicle registration with proof of insurance. Regularly checking expiration dates can help prevent fines and delays. It’s also wise to keep in mind customs rules for importing goods. Many commuters underestimate the limits for fuel or food products, only to run into trouble later. A quick check on the Federal Customs Administration website before crossing can clear up any doubts. Transparency is your best ally: during checks, cooperating with officers and providing accurate information can speed up the process. Cantonal and federal authorities, while conducting controls, are also available to clarify regulations. Remember, the border situation is dynamic and subject to change, especially during peak seasons or in response to specific security needs. To monitor real-time traffic and wait times at border crossings, tools like Viasuisse or dedicated apps can be invaluable. The cross-border worker phenomenon is vital for Ticino’s economy, and collaboration between workers and authorities is essential for a smooth, law-abiding flow. Frequently Asked Questions
How do border crossing controls work in Switzerland?
The border crossing controls in Switzerland are intensified to counter phenomena such as black labor, tax evasion and cross-border crime. Cantonal Police and Confine Guard patrols control the validity of personal documents and vehicles, verifying the identity, Permission G, registration, insurance and maintenance status of vehicles.
What is the impact of the new agreement on the taxation of frontier workers (in force since 2024 for new recruits and from 2026 for frontier workers) on the income statement for frontier workers Switzerland-Italy?
The new agreement requires frontier workers to declare income also in Italy, with Switzerland holding a greater share of taxes. This adds complexity, requiring careful management of tax statements in both countries to avoid double taxation or omissions.
What are the main areas of control intensified on the border between Switzerland and Italy?
The Swiss authorities have concentrated the main controls at the crossings of Brogeda, Stabio-Gaggiolo, Ponte Tresa and Chiasso, with patrols of the Cantonal Police, Guards of Confine, Federal Office of Customs and Security of the borders, and officials of the SUVA.
