Italy inflation April 2026: +2.8% and grocery basket up

Di Valerie Linc · Published on 30 April 2026

In April 2026, Italy's inflation rises to +2.8%, with a +2.5% increase in the grocery basket.

Contesto

In brief - Italy inflation April 2026: +2.8% - Grocery basket: +2.5% - Energy: +9.5% ## Key facts - What: Inflation in Italy - When: April 2026 - Where: Italy - Who: Istat - Amount: +2.8% In April 2026, according to preliminary estimates, inflation in Italy rises to +2.8%. This increase is mainly driven by tensions in energy prices, which register a +9.5% increase compared to -2.1% the previous month. Unprocessed food also shows a significant increase, with a +6.0% compared to +4.7% in March. Istat notes that a partial brake effect on inflation growth is due to the dynamics of prices for certain types of services, including recreational, cultural, and personal care services, which register a +3.0% compared to +2.6% the previous month. Core inflation falls to +1.6% (from +1.9%). The inflation acquired in April, for 2026, is equal to +2.4%. The national index of consumer prices for the entire community (Nic), including tobacco, registers a variation of +1.2% on a monthly basis and +2.8% on an annual basis (from +1.7% the previous month). ### Impact on the grocery basket In April, the annual rate of change of the so-called grocery basket, which includes the prices of food, household, and personal care items, rises to +2.5% (from +2.2%). Prices of high-frequency purchase products also increase, rising from +3.1% to +4.3%. These data highlight a significant impact on inflation for consumers, particularly for those residing in Canton Ticino and working in Italy, where the grocery basket represents a significant part of monthly expenses. ### Comparison with the euro area The annual inflation rate in the euro area is expected to be 3% in April 2026, a significant increase from 2.6% in March, according to a flash estimate by Eurostat. What are the main causes of the increase in inflation in Italy in April 2026?

These increases are partially offset by a more stable dynamic of the prices of some services, such as recreational and cultural ones.
