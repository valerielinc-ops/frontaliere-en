Gender pay gap in Ticino: still a distant goal (cross-border guide)

Di Valerie Linc · Published on 6 May 2026

In Ticino, over 90% of the gender pay gap between men and women has no explanation. Discover the implications for cross-border workers.

Contesto

In brief - Gender pay gap in Ticino: still far away - Average disparity of 16.2% in Switzerland - Over 90% unexplained in Ticino ## Key facts - What: Gender pay gap - When: Data from 2022 and 2024 - Where: Canton Ticino - Who: Cantonal Statistical Office - Amount: 657 CHF less per month for female workers In Ticino, pay equality between men and women remains a distant goal. Despite the Federal Gender Equality Act, which came into force 30 years ago, women still earn 657 CHF less per month than their male colleagues for the same job, same qualifications, and same experience. The gender pay gap affects all professional sectors, from restaurants to hospitals, and does not spare even the most qualified professions. A waitress recounts having to face heavy work without help, while a doctor denounces having done more night shifts than her male colleagues without being able to advance her academic career. A Ticinese employee, on the other hand, saw her career slow down after announcing her pregnancy. The situation in Ticino is particularly severe. At the national level, the average pay gap is 16.2%, but in Ticino, the portion of unexplained salary gap is over 90% in 2022 and 83% in 2024. These data, emerged thanks to an explicit request, show a reality often overlooked. Moreover, the Canton Ticino stands out for the frequency of dismissals after the arrival of a child, with many women receiving a dismissal letter immediately after maternity leave. ### Difficulties in reporting Reporting violations of the Federal Gender Equality Act is often very difficult for victims of pay discrimination. Dettagli operativi

Practical analysis: implications for cross-border workers The wage gap not only affects income but can also have repercussions on other aspects of cross-border workers' lives. For example, a wage gap can influence the ability to obtain social benefits such as AHV and LPP. Additionally, the lack of sanctions for employers who violate the law can make it more difficult for cross-border workers to obtain justice in cases of wage discrimination. ### Comparison before and after the equality law Before the Federal Equality Act came into force, the situation was even worse. Women were often paid less without any justification. After the law came into effect, the situation improved, but not enough. The law introduced mechanisms to report discriminations, but the lack of effective sanctions limited its impact. ### Concrete scenarios Let's imagine two scenarios: a cross-border worker working in Ticino who discovers they earn less than a male colleague for the same job, and another who is fired after announcing a pregnancy. In the first case, the cross-border worker can gather evidence and turn to the Federal Office for Gender Equality. In the second case, the cross-border worker can report the unfair dismissal and seek compensation. However, the process can be long and costly, and the lack of sanctions for the employer can make it difficult to obtain justice. ### Comparisons with other regions In other regions of Switzerland, the situation is different. For example, in the Canton of Zurich, the wage gap is lower and the sanctions for employers who violate the law are more severe. In Ticino, however, the situation is more serious and the sanctions are less effective. What are the tax implications of the gender pay gap?
The gender pay gap can have tax implications, such as difficulties in paying taxes and social contributions. Moreover, the lack of penalties for employers who violate the law can make it harder to obtain compensation and offset tax losses.
