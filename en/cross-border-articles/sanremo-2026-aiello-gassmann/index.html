One of the most anticipated moments of the cover night was undoubtedly the performance by Aiello alongside Leo Gassmann. The two artists delivered an emotional reinterpretation of a classic Italian song, showcasing a harmony that captivated both the audience and critics alike. Aiello, known for blending pop sounds with urban influences, found the perfect partner in Leo Gassmann for a performance filled with energy and emotion. The young Gassmann, winner of the New Proposals category in 2020, brought his warm and powerful voice, while Aiello contributed his unique tone and charismatic stage presence. The result was a cover that honored the original while adding a personal and contemporary touch. The event resonated even in Ticino, where the Sanremo Festival is closely followed by cross-border workers and Italian residents. Italian artists who perform on the Ariston stage are often well-known on this side of the border too, thanks to the strong cultural ties between the two regions. This year, the participation of artists like Aiello and Leo Gassmann further strengthened this bond, drawing attention to one of Italy's most significant musical events.

Dettagli operativi

But what does Sanremo have to do with the world of cross-border workers? The answer lies in the deep cultural and social connection between the Canton of Ticino and Italy. The Festival, besides being a traditional event for millions of Italians, also represents a moment when Italian communities abroad feel closer to their homeland. In Ticino, where cross-border workers make up a significant portion of the workforce, events like Sanremo become an opportunity to celebrate cultural roots and maintain a link with Italian heritage. This connection is also reflected in the region's social and professional landscape. For instance, many cross-border workers commuting to Ticino use public transport to return to Italy and often take the opportunity to share moments of community, discussing events like the Festival. Moreover, the influence of Italian music, amplified by events like Sanremo, is evident in local cultural initiatives organized in the Canton of Ticino, such as the Locarno Festival or musical events in Lugano and Mendrisio. Frequently Asked Questions
How does the cultural link between the Canton of Ticino and Italy work?
The Sanremo Festival represents a moment of cultural connection between the two regions, with many cross-border commuters and Italian residents following the event with great attention. The influence of Italian music is also reflected in local cultural initiatives in the Canton of Ticino, such as the Locarno Festival or the musical events in Lugano and Mendrisio.
What impact does the Sanremo Festival have on the cross-border commuter community in Ticino?
The Sanremo Festival strengthens the cultural and social bond between cross-border commuters and their homeland, offering an opportunity to celebrate Italian cultural roots in Ticino.
How much does public transport cost for cross-border commuters between Italy and Ticino?
The average cost of a monthly pass for public transport between Italy and Ticino is around CHF 120 (about EUR 110 at the current exchange rate of CHF 1 = EUR 0.92). For cross-border commuters, the 'Ticino Ticket' is available at CHF 90 per month, which allows unlimited travel on trains, buses and boats in the border area.
What are the tax differences for cross-border commuters working in Ticino compared to those working in Italy?
Cross-border commuters working in Ticino pay income tax in Switzerland at rates ranging from 5% to 15% depending on income, but are also subject to Italian taxation with tax credit. On average, the overall tax burden is 20-25%, lower than the 30-35% that is paid only in Italy.
How does the CHF-EUR exchange rate affect the net salary of cross-border commuters?
The net salary of a cross-border commuter depends on the CHF-EUR exchange rate. At an exchange rate of 0.92 EUR per 1 CHF, an income of 6,000 CHF per month corresponds to 5,520 EUR. If the franc rises to 0.95 EUR, the same net salary becomes 5,700 EUR, increasing purchasing power in Italy by about 3%.
