Cross-border workers face double payment risk (cross-border guide)

Di Valerie Linc · Published on 13 May 2026

New dispute between Switzerland and Italy over cross-border workers' family allowances. The Federal Council rules out suspending payments.

Contesto

In brief - Clash between Switzerland and Italy over cross-border workers' family allowances - Risk of double payments for cross-border workers - The Federal Council rules out suspension of payments ## Key facts - What: Dispute over cross-border workers' family allowances - When: Since 2022, with the new Italian Universal Single Allowance - Where: Ticino and other border regions - Who: Switzerland and Italy, with the involvement of INPS - Amount: Not specified The Federal Council has rejected the proposal to suspend payments of family allowances to Italian cross-border workers. The decision came in response to a request by Ticino National Councillor Lorenzo Quadri, who had urged a unilateral measure to avoid double payments. The Federal Council clarified that such a suspension would constitute a violation of the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons. However, the main issue remains: without reliable data from INPS, the risk of double payments cannot be excluded. The problem lies with the European platform for sharing information on family allowances, which has been active since October 2021. According to Quadri, the Lombardy section of INPS is negligent in correctly uploading data, unlike other countries. This lack of reliable information exposes Switzerland to the risk of making double or excessive payments. The principle of priority of the state of residence of the offspring requires that, if the family of the cross-border worker already receives an allowance in Italy, Switzerland pays only the difference compared to its own allowance. Dettagli operativi

Analysis of the situation The controversy between Switzerland and Italy over family allowances for cross-border workers has deep roots. Italy's decision to classify the Universal Single Allowance (AUU) as a universal family policy measure, rather than as a pension benefit linked to work, has complicated the exchange of information. According to the Italian interpretation, the AUU is not a benefit that can be coordinated under Regulation (EC) No 883/2004, which means that INPS is not obliged to automatically transmit data to foreign authorities. For Switzerland, however, the AUU should fall under European coordination. Without Italian data, it becomes impossible to determine Switzerland's share or the calculation may be unreliable. This creates a misalignment that could have significant repercussions for cross-border workers. ### Comparison with the previous situation Before the introduction of the Universal Single Allowance in Italy, the information exchange system worked more smoothly. The data necessary for calculating family allowances were regularly transmitted, avoiding double payments. With the new system, however, the lack of transparency and the divergent interpretation of European regulations have created a void that could last a long time. ### Possible scenarios If the situation is not resolved, cross-border workers may have to face a series of complications. These include the need to present additional documents to prove payments already received in Italy, or the possibility of having to repay sums received in excess. Moreover, the lack of clarity could deter some workers from accepting job offers in Switzerland, fearing bureaucratic complications.
