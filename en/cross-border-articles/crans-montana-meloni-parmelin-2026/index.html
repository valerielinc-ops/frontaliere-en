In brief - Meloni-Parmelin meeting in Yerevan - 41 dead and 116 injured in Crans-Montana fire - Italy asks to cover medical expenses ## Key facts - What: Meeting between Meloni and Parmelin - When: May 4, 2026 - Where: Yerevan, Armenia - Who: Giorgia Meloni, Guy Parmelin - Toll: 41 dead, 116 injured The tragedy in Crans-Montana has caused deep sorrow everywhere and created no few misunderstandings between Switzerland and Italy. The President of the Confederation Guy Parmelin and the Italian Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni met on Monday on the sidelines of the European Political Community (EPC) summit in Yerevan, Armenia. The meeting focused on the issue of healthcare costs following the New Year's Eve fire in the bar of the Valais ski resort. The tragedy caused the death of 41 people and injured another 116. Among the victims are also six Italian citizens, while 11 other Italians were injured. Giorgia Meloni has taken up the political commitment of Parmelin so that the families of the Italian girls and boys affected by the tragedy of Crans-Montana are not asked to bear any burden related to the hospital care provided in Switzerland. In recent days, Switzerland had sent bills for the cost of hospital stays to some families of the victims, with the explicit mention that these were not to be paid. Swiss law in this matter indeed provides for a similar procedure to ensure transparency. ### The issue of medical expenses The question of which country will bear the medical expenses remains open. Current regulations provide that Switzerland bills Italy for the healthcare costs of Swiss hospitals for patients residing in Italy and that Italy does the same for Swiss patients who have been able to receive treatment at Niguarda in Milan. The meeting between Giorgia Meloni and Guy Parmelin on the sidelines of the European Political Community (EPC) summit in Yerevan, Armenia, marked an important step towards resolving tensions between Italy and Switzerland regarding healthcare costs for the victims of the Crans-Montana fire. The tragedy, which occurred on December 31, 2025, resulted in the deaths of 41 people and injured another 116, including six Italian citizens who died and eleven who were injured. ### Implications for Cross-Border Workers For cross-border workers who commute to Switzerland and reside in Italy, the issue of healthcare costs is of particular interest. The Swiss procedure of sending bills for hospital care, although provided for by law to ensure transparency, has sparked strong controversy in Italy. The decision not to impose any financial burden on the families of Italian victims represents a positive signal of cooperation between the two countries. ### Comparison with the Previous Situation Before the meeting in Yerevan, the situation was characterized by high tension. The Swiss request to bill medical expenses was perceived as insensitivity towards the victims and their families. The decision to cover medical expenses through aid to victims, to the extent that they are not covered by insurance, represents a significant step forward towards a fair and respectful solution for all parties involved. ### Future Scenarios The question of covering medical expenses for Italian patients treated in Switzerland and vice versa remains open. Current regulations stipulate that Switzerland bills Italy for healthcare costs for patients residing in Italy and that Italy does the same for Swiss patients treated in Italy.
