Committee against mandatory basic dental care insurance: high costs and unresolved issues

Contesto

In brief - Initiative for mandatory dental insurance up for vote on June 14 - Opposing committee: deceptive and costly proposal - Estimated costs between 145 and 150 million annually ## Key facts - What: Initiative for mandatory dental insurance - When: Vote on June 14, 2024 - Where: Canton Ticino - Who: Opposing committee (center-right parties, economic associations, Swiss Society of Dentists Ticino) - Amount: Estimated costs between 145 and 150 million annually The committee opposing the introduction of mandatory insurance for basic dental care, promoted by the left and signed by 8,500 Ticinese, has expressed strong opposition to the proposal. According to the committee, the Swiss dental system works precisely because it is based on prevention. 'A system that works is not changed,' state the center-right parties, economic associations, and the Swiss Society of Dentists Ticino, gathered in Lugano for a unanimous no to the initiative. ### Arguments against the initiative Among the main arguments against the proposal are the costs, which, according to the committee, would increase without solving the problem. 'This is a deceptive initiative,' explained dentist and president of the Society Plinio Rondi. 'So many services, the most expensive ones, which are already today the responsibility of the citizen, would remain so because they do not fall within basic care. Patients would therefore have to pay twice: for the insurance and for the care.' ### Economic consequences According to estimates, the cost of the initiative would amount to between 145 and 150 million annually, compared to the current total of 160 million, 90% of which is borne by patients. Dettagli operativi

• ## Practical Analysis The initiative for mandatory dental insurance raises several practical issues for cross-border workers and residents in Ticino. Let's see what the concrete implications are. ### Additional Costs The introduction of mandatory insurance would result in increased costs for citizens. According to estimates, annual costs would range between 145 and 150 million francs, compared to the current 160 million. This means citizens would have to pay for both the insurance and uncovered treatments, as explained by the president of the Swiss Society of Dentists Ticino, Plinio Rondi. ### Impact on Purchasing Power Funding the proposal, with a 0.8% contribution on the wage bill, would significantly impact the purchasing power of families and businesses. In an already critical economic context, this could represent an additional obstacle to job creation and economic development. ### Comparison with Other Countries The experience of other countries that have introduced similar systems shows that access to care has not improved and costs have increased. This suggests that the initiative might not solve the problems it aims to address but rather worsen the situation. ### Existing Tools There are already targeted tools to help those in difficulty paying for dental care. Collaboration with Soccorso d'inverno, for example, allows identifying and supporting people who do not know they can benefit from these aids. This demonstrates that mandatory insurance is not necessary to ensure access to care. ### Future Scenarios If the initiative were approved, citizens would face additional costs without significant improvement in access to care.
