Foti Law under fire: paper targets only (cross-border guide)

Di Valerie Linc · Published on 8 May 2026

Experts criticize the Foti Law for inefficiencies and distortions, impacting bureaucracy and public administration.

Contesto

In brief - Foti Law criticized for inefficiencies - Dual judicial track penalizes the community - Insurance obligation already postponed ## Key facts - What: Criticism of Foti Law - When: 2026 - Where: Varese, Italy - Who: Experts in administrative law - Amount: Not yet specified The Foti Law, approved in 2026, has been the subject of severe criticism during the ninth Upel Review at Ville Ponti. Three speakers, Vito Tenore, Daniele Granara, and Silvia Torraca, analyzed the law from different perspectives, concluding that the stated goals, such as reducing defensive bureaucracy and boosting the efficiency of public administration, risk remaining only on paper. Tenore focused his intervention on the so-called 'dual track', i.e., the possibility that a public employee may be judged both by the Court of Auditors and by the ordinary civil judge for the same damaging act. This system, already existing in the legal framework, has been transformed into a 'serious pathology' by the Foti reform, according to Tenore. The new law introduces a ceiling on the conviction by the Court of Auditors, equal to 30% of the damage caused and in any case not exceeding twice the annual salary of the employee. This limit does not apply before the civil judge, creating a distortion that, according to Tenore, will inevitably lead to new remissions to the Constitutional Court. Granara focused his intervention on the insurance obligation introduced by the reform, which requires anyone managing public resources to take out a policy covering the patrimonial damages caused by gross negligence. Dettagli operativi

Practical Analysis The Foti law introduces a series of changes that could have a significant impact on the lives of cross-border workers in Ticino. Among the main critical issues is the dual judicial track, which could complicate procedures for transborder workers who have to deal with disputes with public administrations. ### Comparison with the previous situation Before the Foti law, the judicial system for public employees was already complex, but the new reform has introduced further complications. The dual judicial track and the insurance obligation could lead to greater legal uncertainty and increased costs for public administrations, with indirect repercussions on taxpayers and cross-border workers who use public services in Ticino. ### Possible Scenarios If the Foti law is applied as planned, cross-border workers may face more complex procedures to obtain refunds or resolve disputes with public administrations. It is possible that Swiss administrations will have to take more action before the civil court to recover sums not condemned by the Court of Auditors, which could result in longer waiting times for citizens and transborder workers. ### Concrete Procedures Cross-border workers in Ticino should closely monitor the evolution of the Foti law and prepare for any changes in administrative procedures.
