Breast cancer screening at 45: changes in Ticino (cross-border guide)

Di Valerie Linc · Published on 6 May 2026

Three Swiss societies recommend bringing forward breast cancer screening to 45. Here's what changes for women in Ticino.

Contesto

In brief - Early mammography screening at 45 years - 25% reduction in mortality - 16 cantons already with programs at 50 years ## Key facts - What: Early mammography screening - When: May 5, 2026 - Where: Switzerland, including Ticino and Grisons - Who: Swiss Society of Radiology, Gynecology, and Senology - Amount: 25% reduction in mortality The age for accessing breast cancer screening programs in Switzerland should be advanced from 50 to 45 years. The request comes from three medical societies citing new scientific data and the increase in cases among younger women. Supporting the proposal are the Swiss Society of Radiology, the Swiss Society of Gynecology and Obstetrics, and the Swiss Society of Senology. In a joint statement, the three organizations emphasize that starting mammography screening earlier allows for earlier diagnosis, reduces the need for costly and prolonged treatments, and improves the quality of life for patients. ### Implications for women in Ticino Women residing in Ticino and Grisons could benefit from this advancement, as currently, free screening programs begin at 50 years. A possible lowering of the access age would bring Switzerland in line with countries like Germany, Austria, and Sweden, where mammography screening is already recommended or successfully practiced under fifty years. ### Supporting scientific data The recommendation is also based on a recent study according to which regular mammograms starting at 40 years could reduce the mortality rate from breast cancer by about 25%. In the age group between 40 and 49 years, this pathology also represents the second cause of death among women. Practical Analysis: What Changes for Cross-Border Workers Advancing the mammography screening age to 45 could have a significant impact on cross-border workers who work in Ticino and reside in Italy. Currently, women residing in Ticino can access free screening programs starting at age 50. A potential lowering of the access age could lead to greater coverage and earlier diagnosis of breast cancer. ### Comparison with the Current Situation Currently, in Ticino and Grisons, women can access free screening programs starting at age 50. This means that many cross-border workers who work in Switzerland but reside in Italy may need to travel to access these services. Advancing the screening age to 45 could make these services more accessible, reducing the need for frequent travel. ### Economic and Organizational Impact Introducing screening at age 45 would increase costs for Swiss cantons. However, the three Swiss societies emphasize that early diagnosis could reduce the need for costly and prolonged treatments, improving the quality of life for patients and reducing the long-term economic burden. ### Future Scenarios If the advancement of screening to age 45 is approved, women in Ticino and Grisons could see updates to screening programs in the coming years. This change could lead to greater coverage and earlier diagnosis of breast cancer, improving women's health in the region. ### Comparison with Other Countries Countries like Germany, Austria, and Sweden have already advanced mammography screening to age 45, with positive results. Frequently Asked Questions
What are the benefits of bringing forward the mammography screening to 45 years?
Bringing forward the mammography screening to 45 years could lead to early diagnosis of breast cancer, reducing the need for costly and prolonged treatments and improving the quality of life for patients. Additionally, it could reduce the mortality rate for breast cancer by approximately 25%.
What should women in Ticino do to access the screening programs?
Women in Ticino should inform themselves through their doctor or the local screening center, book an appointment, bring the necessary documentation, and follow the instructions of the medical staff.
What are the next steps for the approval of bringing forward the screening?
The three Swiss societies have submitted their recommendation to the Swiss health authorities. The final decision will be up to the cantons, which will need to assess the economic and organizational impact of any potential change.
