Minimum Deductible for Health Insurance: Changes Ahead (cross-border guide)

Di Valerie Linc · Published on 15 March 2026

The Federal Council proposes to raise the minimum deductible from 300 to 400 francs to promote individual responsibility.

The increase of the minimum deductible for health insurance in Switzerland is a significant issue, especially for cross-border workers in Ticino. With a parliamentary motion approved on September 15, 2023, the Federal Council has initiated a consultation to amend the Federal Law on Health Insurance (LAMal), raising the minimum deductible from 300 to 400 francs. This change, which will take effect on January 1, 2025, aims to reduce healthcare costs by encouraging insured individuals to manage their expenses more responsibly. The proposal, which will affect over 45% of adults who opt for the minimum deductible, is seen as a necessary move, as the cost-sharing rate among insured individuals has remained between 13.4% and 13.9% over the past decade. For instance, in the municipality of Lugano, where the average cost of a specialist visit can exceed 200 francs, the increase in the deductible could discourage cross-border workers from seeking medical assistance due to fears of incurring high costs. Dettagli operativi

The adjustment of the minimum deductible will have a direct impact on the insurance policies of cross-border workers and residents in the Canton of Ticino. Currently, the minimum deductible is set at 300 francs, but with the proposal under consultation, an increase to 400 francs is expected. This change requires a thorough analysis of the existing regulations governing health insurance. According to LAMal, the cost-sharing rate for insured individuals should remain below 13.5% of the gross benefits provided by mandatory health insurance. For example, if a cross-border worker receives benefits worth a total of 1,500 francs, with the current deductible, they would have to cover 300 francs, whereas with the proposed increase, they would need to pay 400 francs. This change represents a response to a context of rising healthcare spending, which saw an average increase of 4% in care costs in Ticino in 2022. It could reduce the burden on mandatory health insurance (AOMS) for the government, but at the same time, it places a heavier load on cross-border workers. Cross-border workers, who already face costs related to cross-border employment, such as transportation and housing, will need to consider these changes when planning their finances. The new deductible system is also expected to include a future adjustment mechanism, meaning that deductibles could continue to rise based on healthcare cost trends. First of all, it is advisable to review your insurance policies and assess whether it is appropriate to switch to a higher deductible or maintain the current one. For instance, insurance with a 500 CHF deductible may be more advantageous for those with frequent healthcare needs, while a 2,500 CHF deductible might be more suitable for those who rarely visit a doctor. Frequently Asked Questions
When does the minimum health insurance deductible in Switzerland for frontier workers increase?
The minimum Swiss health insurance deductible will increase from CHF 300 to CHF 400 from 1 January 2025. This amendment, approved by the Federal Council on 15 September 2023, aims to reduce overall healthcare costs and encourage greater responsibility in the management of medical expenses by policyholders.
What will be the impact of the increase in the minimum deductible on access to care for border workers with chronic diseases?
Increasing the minimum allowance to CHF 400 could discourage frontier workers with chronic illnesses from seeking regular care for fear of high expenses, with potential negative long-term health consequences. The government is exploring solutions to avoid compromising access to necessary care.
How much could health insurance premiums in Switzerland for frontier workers decrease with the increase in the deductible?
The adjustment of the minimum deductible is expected to result in a slight decrease in health insurance premiums, estimated at around 2-3% by 2025. This reduction is an attempt to compensate for the increase in cost-sharing for policyholders, but the actual impact on individual border crossers will depend on their specific policy and deductible chosen.
What are the consequences of the increase in the health allowance for frontier workers who receive many medical benefits?
If a frontier worker receives medical benefits for a total value of CHF 1,500, with the minimum allowance increasing from CHF 300 to CHF 400, his cost-sharing share will increase by CHF 100. This increase adds to the costs already incurred for cross-border work, making access to care potentially more onerous.
How will the mechanism for adjusting health allowances in Switzerland for frontier workers change?
The new Swiss deductible system provides for a continuous adjustment mechanism based on the trend in healthcare costs. This means that franchises could increase in the future, not just the minimal one. This is crucial for border workers, who will need to closely monitor future legislative changes and their financial impact.
