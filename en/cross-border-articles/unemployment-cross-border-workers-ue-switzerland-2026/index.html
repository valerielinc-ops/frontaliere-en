Unemployment benefits: EU aligns Swiss workers with residents (cross-border guide)

Di Valerie Linc · Published on 15 May 2026

New EU rules for unemployment benefits: the country of work pays, not the country of residence. What changes for Ticino-Italy cross-border workers.

Contesto

In brief - EU equates cross-border workers and residents for unemployment benefits - The country of employment pays the allowance, not the country of residence - Switzerland is not obliged to follow the new rules ## Key facts - What: Revision of Regulation (EC) No 883/2004 - When: Agreement reached on April 22, 2026, expected to enter into force in July 2026 - Where: European Union - Who: Council of the EU and European Parliament - Amount: Switzerland reimbursements 2025: 283 million CHF to neighboring countries, 600 million CHF received The agreement reached in Brussels overturns decades of practice: in the future, it will be the country where one works — and not where one lives — that will pay the allowance to those who lose their job. Switzerland observes, but is not obliged to follow. A community worker who becomes unemployed in Switzerland could soon count on the same protections as a Swiss citizen. The principle of equal treatment between cross-border workers and residents has received a first formal green light: on April 22, 2026, the delegations of the Council of the EU and the European Parliament reached a provisional agreement within the so-called "trilogue", and on April 29, the Coreper — the Committee of Permanent Representatives of the governments of the EU — approved the text. The norm at the center of the debate is the revision of Regulation (EC) No 883/2004, which coordinates national social security systems. ### What changes for cross-border workers The change of principle: from the place of residence to the place of work. Dettagli operativi

Practical implications for cross-border workers The new EU regulation introduces a significant change for cross-border workers employed in Switzerland. Currently, a cross-border worker residing in Italy and employed in Switzerland receives unemployment benefits from their country of residence, such as INPS. With the reform, however, it will be the country of employment that provides the subsidy, provided that the worker has paid contributions for at least 22 consecutive weeks. ### Comparison between the current situation and the new regulation | Current situation | New regulation | |------------------|----------------| | Benefits paid by the country of residence | Benefits paid by the country of work | | Maximum 5 months reimbursed by Switzerland | No time limit if the worker remains available for the Swiss job market | | Benefits exportable for a maximum of 6 months | Benefits exportable for a maximum of 6 months, extendable | ### What to do if you are a cross-border worker If you are a cross-border worker employed in Switzerland and are interested in understanding the practical implications of this reform, it is important to monitor legislative developments. Concrete steps for cross-border workers If the reform is implemented in Switzerland, cross-border workers will need to follow some steps to access the new benefits. Here’s a practical guide: 1. Check the contributions paid: Ensure you have paid contributions for at least 22 consecutive weeks in the country of work. 2. Register with the URC: If you lose your job, register with the Swiss Regional Employment Center (URC) to access the benefits provided by the Unemployment Insurance Act (LADI). 3. Maintain contact with the URC: If you decide to remain available for the Swiss job market, keep in touch with the URC to avoid time limits on benefits. 4. Export the subsidy: If you decide to return to Italy to look for work, you can export the Swiss subsidy for a maximum of six months, extendable at the discretion of the granting authority. ### Useful tools and resources For further information and to calculate the impact of this reform on your personal situation, you can use the following tools: - Salary calculator: to estimate your net income and taxes. - Guide to unemployment benefits: to better understand your options and rights. - Tax consulting: for personalized assistance. ### Conclusion The EU reform represents a significant change for cross-border workers in Switzerland. Frequently Asked Questions
What changes for cross-border workers with the new EU regulation?
The new EU regulation equates cross-border workers and residents for unemployment benefits. In the future, it will be the country of work that pays the allowance, not the country of residence. Cross-border workers must have paid contributions for at least 22 consecutive weeks in the country of work to access the benefits.
Is Switzerland obliged to follow the new EU rules?
No, Switzerland is not obliged to follow the new EU rules. Any changes require the explicit consent of the Confederation through the Switzerland-EU Joint Committee ALC.
What should you do if you are a cross-border worker and lose your job?
If you are a cross-border worker and lose your job, it is important to register with the Swiss Regional Employment Centre (URC) to access the benefits provided by the Unemployment Insurance Act (LADI). Additionally, it is advisable to consult a tax expert or a labour consultant to better understand your options and rights.
