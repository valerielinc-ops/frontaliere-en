Analysis of Possible Consequences The accusations raised by the editorial could trigger a review of immigration and cross-border work policies between Italy and Switzerland, with a significant impact on Ticino's cross-border workers. In particular, changes could be introduced to the procedures for obtaining the G permit, which is crucial for Italian workers who cross the border every day. Currently, around 65,000 Italian cross-border workers work in Ticino, with about 30,000 in the municipality of Lugano alone. A change in regulations could require a transition period, during which workers should be prepared to present additional documents or meet new conditions. For example, a clause requiring a minimum period of residence in Switzerland before obtaining the G permit could be introduced, similar to what is already provided for other residence permits. ### Comparison with Other Regions Unlike other European regions, Ticino has a particularly high dependence on cross-border work. According to 2023 data, cross-border workers represent about 20% of the Ticino workforce, a significantly higher percentage than other border regions such as the Canton of Geneva (15%) or the Canton of Valais (10%). Diplomatic tensions could therefore have a greater impact here than in other areas. For example, the introduction of quotas for cross-border workers could severely affect municipalities like Mendrisio, where almost 40% of the active population consists of cross-border workers. ### Possible Scenarios One possible scenario is that Swiss authorities introduce new measures to limit the immigration of Italian workers.
