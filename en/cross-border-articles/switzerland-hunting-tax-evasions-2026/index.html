Switzerland widens tax evasion hunt: 110 countries involved

Di Valerie Linc · Published on 3 May 2026

Swiss authorities are also monitoring Swiss citizens in Thailand. Nearly 7,000 foreign assistance requests in 2025.

Contesto

In brief - Switzerland also monitors Swiss citizens in Thailand - Nearly 7,000 requests for assistance from abroad in 2025 - 110 countries involved ## Key facts - What: Exchange of financial information - When: 2025 - Where: Switzerland and 110 countries - Who: Federal Tax Administration - Amount: 3.5 million accounts monitored Switzerland is stepping up its fight against tax evasion by expanding its monitoring network to new countries, including Thailand. According to data from the Federal Tax Administration, in 2025 our country received information relating to 3.5 million accounts and sent 26 countries 61 requests for administrative assistance. This number is down from previous years, but international cooperation remains intense. Adrian Grob, spokesperson for the Federal Tax Administration, specified that most of the requests were directed to Germany and Cyprus. No information exchange, however, with Russia. Requests for clarification mainly come from France, Austria, Germany, Spain, Ukraine, and the United States. Switzerland received 6,968 requests for administrative assistance in 2025, releasing information relating to 3.8 million accounts. This figure marks a decline from the previous year, but remains high compared to the record peak of 2016, when requests exceeded 66,000, mostly from France. ### News and implications Among the novelties are Kenya and Thailand, which have recently joined the system. In particular, 11,000 Swiss citizens reside in Thailand, who will be closely monitored. The hunt for undeclared funds is a global challenge, with Switzerland continuing to strengthen its international cooperation to combat tax evasion. Dettagli operativi

The hunt for undeclared funds is a priority for Switzerland, which is strengthening its collaboration with other countries to combat tax evasion. This international commitment has significant implications for Swiss citizens living abroad, including cross-border workers who work in Switzerland. ### Implications for cross-border workers Cross-border workers who work in Switzerland must be particularly attentive to new tax monitoring measures. With the extension of the information exchange network to new countries, such as Thailand, it is crucial to ensure compliance with all Swiss and Italian tax regulations. Double taxation can be avoided thanks to bilateral agreements, but it is essential to be transparent and provide all the information requested by the tax authorities. ### Concrete procedures To avoid tax problems, cross-border workers should follow these procedures: 1. Income tax return: Ensure that all income earned in Switzerland and Italy is correctly declared. 2. Information exchange: Be aware that financial information can be exchanged between the countries involved. 3. Tax advice: Consult a tax advisor to ensure compliance with all regulations and to optimize your tax situation. 4. Account monitoring: Keep track of all bank accounts and financial transactions to be prepared for any requests for clarification. ### Concrete scenario Suppose a Swiss cross-border worker residing in Italy has a bank account in Switzerland.
