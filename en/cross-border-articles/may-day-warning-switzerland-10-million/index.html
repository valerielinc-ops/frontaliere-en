In brief - Thousands protest against UDC initiative - Unions and politics united to defend workers' rights - Vote on the initiative on June 14 ## Key facts - What: Protests against the UDC initiative 'No to a Switzerland of 10 million!' - When: May 1, 2026 - Where: Zurich and other Swiss cities - Who: Unions, politics, citizens - Attendance: Around 15,000 people in Zurich On May 1, 2026, Switzerland saw widespread mobilization in defense of workers' rights and a strong warning against the popular initiative of the Swiss People's Party (SVP, conservative right) 'No to a Switzerland of 10 million!', which will be put to a vote on June 14. Thousands of people took to the streets in various locations (Zurich first and foremost, as per tradition), with the participation of union leaders and two federal councillors. The Labor Day celebrations were an opportunity for the union to express concern about the potential consequences of the SVP initiative. Pierre-Yves Maillard, president of the Swiss Trade Union Federation (SGB) and socialist councillor of the states of Vaud, stated that the initiative represents a concrete threat to wages, pensions and jobs. 'We do not want a Switzerland where workers can come, but at the price of separation from their families. We oppose the abolition of wage controls, wage dumping and black work', Maillard said. ### Implications for cross-border workers The SVP initiative, if approved, could have a significant impact on cross-border workers in Switzerland. The limitation of the number of active people could make it more difficult to find work, especially in sectors already suffering from a labor shortage. Practical analysis: what changes for cross-border workers The UDC initiative, if approved, could have a significant impact on cross-border workers employed in Switzerland. Limiting the number of active individuals could make it harder to find work, especially in sectors already facing labor shortages. Additionally, the abolition of wage controls could lead to a decrease in salaries, with negative consequences for transborder workers. ### Before/after comparison Before the approval of the initiative, cross-border workers could count on a series of rights and protections, including wage controls and the possibility of working in Switzerland without having to leave their families. After approval, however, they might face a series of difficulties, such as the need to find work in an increasingly restricted market and the possibility of having to accept lower salaries. ### Concrete scenarios If the initiative were approved, Switzerland might have to renounce the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons with the European Union, putting the bilateral path at risk. This could lead to an increase in immigration barriers and greater difficulty for cross-border workers in finding jobs in Switzerland. Furthermore, the limitation of immigration could worsen the shortage of qualified personnel, particularly in the healthcare sector, which is heavily dependent on foreign workers. ### Concrete procedures For cross-border workers who wish to work in Switzerland, it is important to be aware of the possible consequences of the UDC initiative. What are the main concerns of the unions regarding the UDC initiative?
Unions are concerned about the potential consequences of the UDC initiative on wages, pensions, and jobs. In particular, they fear that the initiative could lead to a reduction in salaries, greater job insecurity, and a decrease in worker protections, especially for cross-border workers.
What could happen if the UDC initiative is approved?
If the UDC initiative is approved, Switzerland might have to renounce the Agreement on the free movement of persons with the European Union, putting the bilateral path at risk. This could lead to an increase in immigration barriers and greater difficulty for cross-border workers in finding jobs in Switzerland. Furthermore, the limitation of immigration could worsen the shortage of qualified personnel, especially in the healthcare sector, which is heavily dependent on foreign workers.
