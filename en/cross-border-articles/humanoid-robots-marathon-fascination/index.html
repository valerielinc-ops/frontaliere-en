Humanoid robots win half-marathon: progress or fascination?

Di Valerie Linc · Published on 29 April 2026

A humanoid robot has broken the world record for the half-marathon in Beijing, sparking debates on the usefulness and ethics of these technologies

Contesto

In brief - Humanoid robot sets world record in half marathon in Beijing - Record time: 50 minutes and 26 seconds - Philosopher Francesca Rigotti: "We don't need robots that run" ## Key facts - What: Humanoid robot sets world record in half marathon - When: April 19, 2026 - Where: Beijing, China - Who: Robot developed by Honor (Huawei subsidiary) - Time: 50 minutes and 26 seconds for 21 km In Beijing, for the first time, humanoid robots have beaten human athletes in a half marathon. The winning robot, developed by the Chinese brand Honor (a subsidiary of Huawei), completed the 21 kilometers in 50 minutes and 26 seconds, beating the world record set just one month ago by Ugandan Jacob Kiplimo in Lisbon. A significant leap from 2025, when most robots had not even managed to cross the finish line. ### Utility or fascination? The news quickly went viral worldwide, but beyond the stopwatch and technology, one might wonder how necessary it is to have a humanoid robot run such a distance. "We need many things in the world today, but humanoid robots running the fastest marathon... I would say no," observes philosopher and essayist Francesca Rigotti, commenting on the news. "These humanoids can be evaluated as very expensive toys. But to say that we need them, that they are necessary, I would say no." It's a simple, almost disarming response, but it immediately shifts the focus of the discussion from pure technology to a somewhat more abstract but profound concept. Utility or ancient fascination? Engineers often tell us that this type of robot can have concrete industrial applications. However, the question is whether these robots effectively solve real human problems. Practical Applications and Implications for Cross-Border Workers The technology of humanoid robots could have concrete implications in various sectors, including elderly care and healthcare. However, as Francesca Rigotti points out, the risk is that technology, from being a useful tool, might gradually shift towards a replacement that impoverishes human relationships. "We wouldn't like to end up in a nursing home, let alone see ourselves being brought medicine or a bowl of soup in the evening by a nurse with a little dress, knowing it's a robot," says the philosopher. "Perhaps the first time it will amuse us, surprise us, and we'll say: how nice! But the second and third time, we'll look for a hand, and I would say a warm hand. It will never be the same thing." In an aging society, the issue of elderly care becomes increasingly concrete. Cross-border workers employed in the healthcare sector in Switzerland might find themselves having to deal with these new technologies in their daily work. It is important to consider how these innovations might influence the relationship between healthcare workers and patients, as well as the impact on the quality of life of the elderly. ### Future Scenarios According to the philosopher, seeing these robots in everyday life is not at all a distant hypothesis. Ten years? Perhaps even less. The presence of the humanoid is not just a matter of wonder, but also of responsibility. When a machine makes decisions or acts in real contexts, ethics must be considered.
