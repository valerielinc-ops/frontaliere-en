Operational details

To better understand how fuel prices are composed in Switzerland, it’s essential to analyze the various components that influence the final cost. Under current regulations, the price at the pump is made up of several elements: the crude oil cost, federal and cantonal taxes, excise duties, distribution expenses, and profit margins. The international price of oil, which recently hit peaks above $80 per barrel, accounts for roughly 60% of the total cost. Taxes—including VAT and excise duties—contribute about 25%, while the remaining portion covers distribution costs and the profit margins of oil companies. In Switzerland, excise taxes are set at the federal level, whereas cantonal taxes vary slightly across regions, affecting prices in border areas like Ticino. The Swiss system benefits from preferential taxation on fuels, but international price hikes threaten to undermine these advantages. Market volatility, geopolitical tensions, and fluctuations in the euro-franc exchange rate all amplify price increases, making future trends difficult to predict. It’s important to note that during unstable periods, oil companies tend to raise prices more quickly, while authorities—both local and national—may consider intervention measures. For cross-border workers, understanding this breakdown helps in planning refueling strategies and finding ways to save. For example, if today’s pump price is 2.10 CHF, about 1.26 CHF of that can be attributed to crude oil costs and taxes, with the rest representing margins and distribution.
