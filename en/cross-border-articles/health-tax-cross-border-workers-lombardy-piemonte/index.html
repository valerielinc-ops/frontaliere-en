Lombardy demands health tax alignment, Piedmont says no

Di Valerie Linc · Published on 1 May 2026

Lombardy insists on applying the health tax to cross-border workers, while Piedmont confirms its opposition. Here's what changes

Contesto

In brief - Lombardy confirms the application of the health tax to cross-border workers with operational details - Piedmont reiterates its opposition, creating a regulatory misalignment - The tax will come into effect from September 2026, with an estimated impact on 70,000 cross-border workers ## Key facts - What: Health tax for cross-border workers - When: From September 2026 - Where: Lombardy - Who: Lombardy Region - Amount: Estimated between 100 and 300 euros annually, depending on income The turbulent session of the regional council in Lombardy defined the first details of the controversial health tax, with payments starting from September 2026. The rule provides for a levy on cross-border workers to increase the salaries of border healthcare personnel. This measure has sparked various reactions, with the Pd, through councilor Angelo Orsenigo, reiterating the absolute opposition and illegitimacy of the rule. Marco Contessa, national responsible for cross-border workers for the Cisl, underlined how this measure could create tensions between cross-border workers and healthcare personnel. The situation further complicates with Piedmont's position, which has repeatedly stated, through the undersecretary to the presidency of Piedmont Region Alberto Preioni, not to want to apply such a rule. Despite the recall of the regional councilor of Fratelli d'Italia Giacomo Zamperini, who underlined how it would be unacceptable for other regions such as Piedmont not to apply it, Preioni reiterated Piedmont's opposition. Dettagli operativi

Comparison between Lombardy and Piedmont The decision by Lombardy to apply the health tax to cross-border workers, starting from January 1, 2024, creates a regulatory gap with Piedmont, which has chosen not to adopt the same measure. This could lead to situations of disparity among cross-border workers employed in the two regions. Lombardy, with over 70,000 cross-border workers primarily from Ticino, is seeking to increase the salaries of healthcare personnel through a 1.4% levy on the gross annual salary, with a maximum cap of 3,000 euros. Piedmont, which hosts approximately 30,000 cross-border workers, has decided not to follow Lombardy's example, creating tensions and discussions between the two regions. ### Possible Scenarios If Piedmont maintains its position, cross-border workers employed in Lombardy could find themselves at a disadvantage compared to those working in Piedmont. For example, a cross-border worker residing in Lugano and working in Milan will pay the health tax, while a colleague working in Turin will not be subject to this levy. This could lead to an increase in requests for clarification and a possible intervention by the competent authorities to ensure fair treatment. It is crucial that cross-border workers stay updated on the latest news and prepare for any changes in regulations. ### Concrete Procedures Cross-border workers employed in Lombardy should start informing themselves about the specific procedures for paying the health tax. It is advisable to contact the competent offices to obtain detailed information on the required documents and deadlines.
