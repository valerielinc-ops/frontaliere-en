Chiasso: The Community Rediscovers Itself Between Faith and New Values (cross-border guide)

Di Valerie Linc · Published on 2 March 2026

A changing Chiasso: parish priest Don Feliciani reveals a paradigm shift in sacraments, with adults choosing confirmation at 40. A signal for cross-border commuters.

Contesto

TL;DR - Chiasso undergoes social and spiritual transformation - Don Feliciani notes shift in sacraments approach - Cross-border commuters seek deeper integration ## Key facts - Cosa: Transformation in Chiasso's social and spiritual fabric - Quando: Just before Don Feliciani's retirement - Chi: Don Gianfranco Feliciani, historic Chiasso parish figure - Importo: 25 years of service in the Chiasso parish - Dove: Chiasso, a vibrant border town in Ticino - Fatto: Sacraments now chosen by adults out of personal motivation - Esempio: Confirmation received at age 40 by choice Chiasso, the vibrant border town that thousands of cross-border commuters pass through daily, is undergoing a profound transformation in its social and spiritual fabric. It's not just about economic figures or changes in traffic flow, but a more intimate shift concerning the sense of community and approach to traditional values. Just a few months before his retirement, Don Gianfranco Feliciani, a historic figure in the Chiasso parish for 25 years, made an observation that resonates far beyond the church walls, touching on the very essence of integration and Ticinese identity in a global context. His revelation, reported by Ticinonline, is surprising: 'Christianity doesn't work the way it used to. Today, sacraments are no longer obtained because 'one has to.' But because one wants to. And indeed, I have people who receive confirmation at 40. By choice.'

Dettagli operativi

For cross-border commuters who cross the border daily to contribute to the Ticinese economy, Chiasso's social transformation offers fundamental food for thought. The conscious choice of a sacrament in adulthood, rather than out of mere tradition, symbolizes a more proactive approach to belonging and identity. This 'change in approach' is particularly relevant in a context like Ticino, where the coexistence of different cultures and origins is the norm. Cross-border commuters are not just a workforce; many seek a deeper integration into the social fabric, desiring to feel part of the Ticinese community beyond mere employment. Punti chiave

For cross-border commuters, understanding these social dynamics is not a purely academic exercise but a practical component for a better quality of life and more effective integration into the Canton of Ticino. Living on the border doesn't just mean crossing it for work but also immersing oneself in local realities, understanding their evolutions, and contributing to their development. Chiasso, with its openness and ability to re-imagine faith and community, offers a tangible example of how values can be rediscovered and strengthened through individual choice. This approach is particularly valuable for those navigating between two cultures, offering a model for finding one's place in a new context, not out of obligation but through personal motivation.
