Self-management spaces in Lugano: Ghisletta, 'The topic is not on the table' (cross-border guide)

Di Valerie Linc · Published on 4 May 2026

Work begins on the redevelopment of Lugano's former slaughterhouse, while discussions continue on the future use of the former Viganello sewing school for independent culture.

Contesto

In brief - Work begins on the former Lugano Slaughterhouse - Ghisletta: 'No specific discussions planned for self-management' - Future former Viganello sewing school could host independent culture ## Key facts - What: Redevelopment of the former Lugano Slaughterhouse - When: Work began this morning - Where: Lugano - Who: Raoul Ghisletta, head of real estate for the City of Lugano - Duration: Work estimated to last between a month and a half and two months A new chapter has begun for the former Lugano Slaughterhouse. Work on the redevelopment of the area began this morning, marking the definitive end of a symbolic place for Ticino self-management, which still seems 'homeless', as explained to Ticinonews by Raoul Ghisletta, head of real estate for the City of Lugano. 'On this topic, we have no specific discussions planned.' The situation is different regarding independent culture. 'In this sense, there are discussions, particularly concerning the future former Viganello sewing school, which will move to Chiasso in 2028: if an agreement is reached with the Canton, since the building is canton-owned, it could be used for so-called independent culture.' ### Implications for cross-border workers The redevelopment of the former Lugano Slaughterhouse and the possible allocation of the future former Viganello sewing school to independent culture could have a significant impact on the community of cross-border workers living and working in Ticino. These spaces could offer new cultural and social opportunities, improving the quality of life and integration of cross-border workers into the local community. Dettagli operativi

Procedure for the Redevelopment of the Former Slaughterhouse The overall duration of the operation started this morning is estimated between a month and a half and two months, after which the work will move inside the building to free the walls and allow architects to verify the state of the art of the building. 'It is a protected monument, so it must be maintained and restored,' Ghisletta points out, recalling that 'for this reason it is important to be able to ascertain the exact conditions of the building.' Subsequently, the matter will return to the municipal council. 'A credit will have to be voted on to design the interior spaces and one for the construction.' In terms of timing, 'the first step could arrive next year, while the work could start in two or three years.' ### Practical Implications for Cross-Border Workers Cross-border workers interested in participating in the cultural and social life of the Canton of Ticino should closely monitor the development of these projects.
