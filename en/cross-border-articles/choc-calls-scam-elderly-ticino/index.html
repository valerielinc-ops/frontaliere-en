Frequently Asked Questions
What are the characteristics of the 'shock phone calls' that are affecting the elderly in Ticino?
'Shock phone calls' are urgent and intimidating calls, often from alleged officials of banks, law enforcement or public bodies, which induce victims to make immediate payments via bank transfer, prepaid cards or phone top-ups.
What is the recommended action protocol for an elderly person who receives a suspicious phone call in Ticino?
The recommended procedure is: 1) Do not provide information, 2) Interrupt the call, 3) Report to 147 or to the website of the Ticino Cantonal Police, 4) Direct verification with the institution via official number.
How can I check if a suspicious phone call is a scam before providing data?
To assess a suspicious call, do not provide information immediately and call the official number of the indicated entity (e.g. bank or police) using the contacts on the website. Check caller ID with your carrier's app, report the call to 147, and keep the call log for investigation.
What penalties does Ticino law provide for those who carry out telephone scams on the elderly?
Ticino's penalties for telephone scams include fines of up to 50,000 francs and imprisonment of two to five years, according to Article 146 of the Criminal Code updated in 2022. The ordinance of 1 January 2022 strengthened the penalties, also providing for the seizure of illegally obtained assets.
What should I do if I have already sent money to a scammer via wire transfer?
If you have already made a transfer to a scammer, contact your bank immediately to block the operation and request a refund, providing all the details of the transaction. File a report on the toll-free number 147 or at the nearest police station and keep the evidence (screenshots, bank statements) to facilitate the investigation.
