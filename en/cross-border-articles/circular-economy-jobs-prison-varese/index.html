Circular economy repairs lives: two prison work stories (cross-border guide)

Di Valerie Linc · Published on 7 May 2026

Two projects in Varese and Busto Arsizio prisons transform waste into job opportunities for inmates, reducing recidivism and creating social value.

Contesto

In brief - Two prison projects turn waste into job opportunities - Made in Carcere reduces recidivism to almost zero - Officina Creativa creates bags and accessories with recycled materials ## Key facts - What: Circular economy projects in prison - When: Since 2007 for Made in Carcere - Where: Lecce and Busto Arsizio prisons - Who: Luciana Delle Donne and Roberto Grassi - Cost: 60,000 euros per year, the cost of a prisoner Luciana Delle Donne, former finance manager, left everything to dedicate herself to a project that has revolutionized the lives of many imprisoned women. Her project, Made in Carcere, was born in 2007 with the aim of transforming fashion waste into precious materials for sewing workshops run by imprisoned women. During the CSR and Social Innovation Fair, held on Wednesday, May 6th at the Centro Congressi Ville Ponti in Varese, Delle Donne told her story and the success of the project. The mechanism is simple: major fashion houses donate their waste, which in prison workshops becomes bags, cases, and keychains. Each piece is unique, made with different materials and worked by different hands. The workshop employs about ten people, with peaks of thirty during busy periods. The turnover is high, but the results are impressive: over five hundred people have passed through Made in Carcere's workshops in twenty years, with almost zero recidivism, compared to an average of 70% for those who go to prison. 'What fashion discards becomes a precious asset for us,' explains Delle Donne. 'Through creativity and color matching, these women exist, because choosing means existing.'
