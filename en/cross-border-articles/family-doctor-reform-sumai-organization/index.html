Family doctor reform: Sumai, the issue is organization and lack of professionals

Di Valerie Linc · Published on 27 April 2026

Antonio Magi, general secretary of Sumai-Assoprof, contests the idea of dependency of family doctors, highlighting organizational problems and lack of professionals.

Contesto

In brief - Sumai-Assoprof challenges the reform of general practitioners - The issue is organizational, not contractual - Risks: loss of trust relationship, waiting lists, higher costs ## Key facts - What: Reform of general practitioners - When: April 27, 2026 - Where: Italy - Who: Sumai-Assoprof - Amount: Not specified Antonio Magi, general secretary of Sumai-Assoprof, the union of outpatient specialists, challenged the idea that the reform of general practitioners should focus on dependency. According to Magi, the main problem is not the contract but the organization and the shortage of professionals. 'It is not necessary to change the legal relationship of doctors, but to apply existing agreements, clarify roles and respect times,' Magi declared. The knot, according to him, is organizational: insufficient planning and shortage of professionals. The proposal to introduce forms of dependency in general medicine is presented as a modern and efficient solution. However, Magi warns that it could produce effects opposite to those declared. Among the risks indicated: the loss of the trust relationship between doctor and patient, the creation of waiting lists to access the general practitioner and an exponential increase in healthcare costs. ### Implications for cross-border workers The reform could have a significant impact on cross-border workers who work in Ticino and reside in Lombardy. Dettagli operativi

Practical Analysis The proposed reform of general practitioners could have a significant impact on cross-border workers who work in Ticino and reside in Lombardy. The loss of the trust relationship between doctor and patient could make it more difficult for cross-border workers to access healthcare, as they often have to manage their health between two different healthcare systems. Additionally, the creation of waiting lists could worsen the situation, making it even more complicated for cross-border workers to access necessary care in a timely manner. ### Comparison with the Current Situation Currently, the contracted model guarantees immediate access to care and real follow-up. The transition to a scheduled system, bound by shifts, objectives, and organized activities, would inevitably introduce a time filter. This means that citizens, including cross-border workers, would no longer have direct access to the doctor but would enter a regulated path, moving from a proximity system to a waiting system. ### Possible Scenarios If the reform goes ahead as proposed, cross-border workers could find themselves facing longer waiting times for medical visits, with consequent delays in diagnoses and treatments. Furthermore, the increase in healthcare costs could lead to higher taxes or health contributions, directly impacting the wallets of cross-border workers. ### What to Do Cross-border workers who are concerned about the impact of the reform on their health and wallet should closely monitor the development of the situation. It is important to stay informed and participate in public debates to make their voices heard. Additionally, cross-border workers should consider the possibility of taking out supplementary health insurance to ensure timely access to medical care....
