Dettagli operativi

The impact of social media on democracy is a highly topical issue, so much so that the World Economic Forum included disinformation spread through social media among the top threats to global stability in its 2021 Global Risks Report. As social media usage grows, so does concern about its impact on democracy. Switzerland, for example, was involved in the Magnitsky case, a vast money-laundering scheme originating in Russia, which highlighted how democracy is under increasing pressure — though it is not always clear when, or if, democracy truly 'collapses'. Social media can influence democracy in several ways. One of the most alarming is the spread of false news, or fake news, which can sway public opinion and manipulate elections. For instance, according to a 2020 report by the Council of Europe, online disinformation campaigns peaked during the 2016 US presidential elections, with over 200 million manipulated tweets shared worldwide. In Switzerland, the 2014 immigration referendum demonstrated how online disinformation campaigns can influence the outcome of a popular vote. Another way social media can influence democracy is through the manipulation of public opinion. Influencer marketing campaigns, for example, can be used to subtly spread political messages without users even realizing it. Additionally, social media algorithms can create filter bubbles, where users only see content that confirms their existing beliefs, limiting exposure to different viewpoints and hindering democratic debate. To counter these threats, a holistic approach involving all stakeholders is needed — from social platforms to governments, from non-governmental organizations to individual citizens. Punti chiave

Information wars pose an increasing threat to democracy, especially in an era where social media has such a vast influence on public opinion formation. To protect democracy from negative social media influences, it's important to be aware of news sources and potential biases. Additionally, promoting media literacy and the education of informed citizens is crucial. In Ticino, for example, the issue of immigration has often been a hot topic in media and social media. According to a 2020 survey, 65% of Ticinese people get their news online, with social media being the primary source for 35% of respondents. However, only 15% of respondents reported being able to distinguish between true and false online news. This highlights the importance of being aware of news sources and potential biases. To protect democracy from negative social media influences, it's important to follow some operational checklists. First, verify the source of the news. There are many websites and social media accounts that spread false or misleading news. It's important to ensure that news comes from reliable and trustworthy sources. Second, be aware of potential biases. Media can have political or ideological biases that can influence news coverage. It's important to seek out different sources and compare news to get a more complete picture of the situation. Finally, promote media literacy and the education of informed citizens. There are many organizations dedicated to educating informed citizens, such as the Citizenship Education Foundation (FIC) in Ticino. FIC offers courses and workshops to help citizens distinguish between true and false news and understand the mechanisms of information.
